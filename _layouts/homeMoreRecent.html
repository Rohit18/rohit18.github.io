I am a postdoctoral researcher specializing in the application of machine learning and deep learning techniques in geospatial analysis. Currently. working on projects related to urban flooding, informal road detection in Central America, global surface water detection, and assessing the impact of humanitarian assistance programs. I enjoy extracting meaningful information from satellite imagery with a focus on the drivers of climate change and their impacts. I am working at the social [pixel] lab (University of Arizona) with the simply brilliant Dr. Beth Tellman.

Urban Flooding

A NASA Terrestrial Hydrology Project

We are trying to address issues related to urban flooding, rather than flooding in general. Why? Because it is more difficult to map urban floods due to complex and heterogeneous landscape. We are attempting to predict a daily flood extent map for the entire flooding duration. This requires fusing data from multiple sources, each with different spatial, spectral, temporal resolutions and measurement principles (optical and radar). We built a hand-labeled dataset using PlanetScope to capture urban flooding in 14 cities across the world called the Urban Flood Dataset (maybe we will come up with a better name for it).

A sample from our training dataset built using Labelbox. Source: Planet.

Informal Roads in Central America

A NASA LCLUC Project. Working with and learning a lot from Nicholas Magglioca, Matt Fagan, Steven Sesnie, Erik Nielsen, Jennifer Devine, and Kendra McSweeney.

Narco-trafficking is a driver of deforestation in the protected forests in Central America. Markers of narco-trafficking are informal roads. We first developed a hand-labeled dataset to label roads and buildings. We found that our deep learning model trained on the hand-labeled dataset identified roads better than Microsoft and OSM combined. Not a surprise as its a local model with local data, nevertheless, quite cool. However, we are also interested in going back in time till 2001 using Landsat 7 (nototious for its SLC-off issue) to look at road growth over time. We are combining different sources of data to build a counter-factual model to measure the impact of narco-trafficking on deforestation.

Markers of narco-trafficking in Central America. Sources: Planet and ESA.

Global Surface Water Mapping

A NASA ACCESS Project. Enjoying working with Fritz Pollicelli.

We need validation datasets to evaluate the hundreds (thousands?) of surface water mapping methods using satellite imagery. We built a globally sampled dataset using PlanetScope imagery. 90 samples from all the Biomes. Currently building yet another deep learning model with a focus on sampling rather than the method with the help of the impressive SWORD dataset.

Our 90 globally distributed samples overlayed on the different biomes. Source: World Wildlife Fund (WWF) Global Ecoregions Map

Desert Floods in Arizona

A Pima County Flood (Arizona) project.

Keeping it local. We tried to map the fast moving floods in the Sonoran desert and found how difficult it is (hardly any literature on it too). Instead of surface water we looked for wet soils immediately (next day around 10:30am) after a rainfall using PlanetScope imagery and comapared them to previous dry images to measure flood extent. We examined the locations of road closures and identified locations where there should be road closures next time there was flooding.

Identifying regions where it flooded, but there weren't any road closures for safety. Source: Planet.

Tools

Geospatial Technologies: I work with geospatial python libraries like rasterio, geopandas, and geemap all the time. GDAL is my favorite (huge shout out to Spatial Thoughts by Ujaval Gandhi). When I need to visualize, I use QGIS, Google Earth Engine, and Microsoft Planetary. To download spatial data, I use GEE, NASA CMR, and Hyp3 Working with geospatial data means that I have to troubleshoot with almost every new dataset I work with, but I have those in our lab's private repos for now. Nothing works out of the box so I have incredible respect for folks who build products with geospatial data.

Machine Learning Tools: I learned deep learning using Keras and Tensforflow (with help from Andrew Ng and Andrej Karpathy's materials) many years ago, but Fastai with Pytorch quickly became my preferred library due to their simpicity. Thank you, Jeremy Howard and Rachel Thomas.

Development Environments: I love coding with vscode (with the help of Copilot-X, which helps me try new things) and sometimes use Jupyter Notebooks (mostly to communicate findigs).

Computational Platforms: When training larger models, I use Google Colab (initial trial runs), Lambdabox (our lab has 2 3080s), High-Performance Computing (HPC) from UofA, and occasionally Alaska Science Facility's OpenScienceLab.

Image Annotation Tools: I spent some time annotating images using Labelbox and NASA ImageLabeler. Labelbox has more features but NASA ImageLabeler is quite impressive in itself.
